**Questions and answers:**
1. **What is asymmetric encryption and how does it work in GPG? Difference between encrypting and signing a message.**
Asymmetric encryption uses a public key to encrypt and a private key to decrypt, allowing secure communication without sharing a password beforehand (Zimmermann, 1995). In GPG, a message is encrypted using the recipient’s public key so only they can read it. A message is signed using the sender’s private key to prove it came from them and has not been modified (Garfinkel, 2003).
2. **Compare ProtonMail, Gmail and Outlook in terms of encryption and privacy.**
ProtonMail provides end-to-end encryption by default and uses zero-access encryption, meaning the provider cannot read user emails (Proton Technologies, 2026). In contrast, Gmail and Outlook encrypt data in transit and at rest, but they control the encryption keys, which allows them to access user data if required by law (Google, 2025; Microsoft, 2025).
3. **What is the PKI model based on Certificate Authorities (CA)? Cite 2 advantages and 2 disadvantages compared to Web of Trust.**
The PKI model uses Certificate Authorities (CAs) to verify identities and issue digital certificates (Chokhani et al., 2003). Its advantages include scalability and standardized identity verification. However, its disadvantages are that a compromised CA can break trust globally and that users must trust many authorities without direct verification (Wilson, 1998).
4. **Investigate EU GDPR and Ecuador's Data Protection Law. What rights do they give citizens regarding email and digital surveillance?**
The GDPR gives EU citizens rights such as access, deletion, and data portability, as well as the right to object to automated surveillance (European Parliament, 2016). Ecuador’s data protection law grants rights like access and deletion of personal data, but it does not clearly include data portability or strong protections against digital surveillance (Asamblea Nacional del Ecuador, 2021; Ramos Villavicencio et al., 2025).
5. **What is email metadata? Why doesn't content encryption protect metadata, and what privacy implications does this have?**
Email metadata includes information such as the sender, recipient, time, and IP address found in email headers. Content encryption like GPG does not protect metadata because email servers need this information to deliver messages (Nurse et al., 2015). As a result, even encrypted emails can still reveal communication patterns, which affects user privacy
